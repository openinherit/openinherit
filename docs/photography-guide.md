# Photography Guide for Estate Items

Photographing items for an estate inventory does not require professional equipment — a modern smartphone is more than adequate. What matters is capturing the right views of each item so that valuers, dealers, and executors can identify, grade, and appraise without handling the physical object.

This guide explains the INHERIT `viewType` system and provides per-category recommendations for what to photograph, how to light it, and what dealers actually need to see.

---

## viewType Reference

Every photograph attached to an asset in INHERIT carries a `viewType` tag. These 12 values cover the full range of documentary needs:

| viewType | Purpose | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| `overview` | Full item in context, showing overall form and size | Every item — this is the primary identification shot |
| `identification` | Key identifying feature: model name, title, label | When the item has a name plate, title page, dial, or other identity feature |
| `condition` | Overall condition — wear, patina, completeness | Every item — shows general state |
| `provenance` | Proof of origin or ownership history: receipts, gallery labels, dedications | When the item has labels on the reverse, inscriptions, or documentation |
| `maker_mark` | Manufacturer stamp, hallmark, signature, foundry mark | When a maker's mark is visible |
| `serial_number` | Serial number, model number, or unique reference engraved/printed on the item | When a serial or model number is present |
| `damage` | Close-up of specific damage: chips, tears, cracks, stains, missing parts | When damage exists that affects value |
| `scale_reference` | Item photographed next to a ruler, coin, or other known-size object | When size is not obvious from context (small items, miniatures) |
| `label` | Label, sticker, or printed information on packaging or the item itself | When a label carries catalog numbers, edition info, or content details |
| `certificate` | Accompanying certificate, valuation, or grading report | When a certificate of authenticity, grading slab, or valuation exists |
| `receipt` | Purchase receipt or invoice | When a receipt is available and legible |
| `environment` | Storage or display conditions — shelving, cases, cellar conditions | When storage quality is relevant to value (wine, art, instruments) |

## Per-Category Recommendations

### Model Railways

**Essential viewTypes:** `overview`, `identification`, `condition`, `label`, `serial_number`

- **Overview:** Photograph the locomotive or rolling stock on a plain surface in good daylight. Include the box if available — for many collectors, the box is half the value.
- **Identification:** Capture the running number on the cab side and the manufacturer name on the underframe.
- **Label:** Photograph the box end-flap showing the catalogue number, item description, and barcode.
- **Condition:** Show any paint chips, broken couplings, missing parts, or discolored decals.
- **Lighting:** Indirect daylight or a desk lamp bounced off a white surface. Avoid direct flash — it creates harsh reflections on gloss paintwork.
- **Background:** Plain white or light gray. Avoid patterned surfaces that distract from detail.
- **What dealers need:** The catalogue number (from the box or base), the livery variant, whether it has been run, and whether DCC is fitted.

### Watches

**Essential viewTypes:** `overview`, `serial_number`, `maker_mark`, `condition`, `certificate`

- **Overview:** Lay the watch face-up on a dark, non-reflective surface. Include the bracelet or strap fully extended.
- **Serial number:** For Rolex: photograph between the lugs at 6 o'clock (remove the bracelet if possible) or the rehaut engraving. For Omega: the case back.
- **Maker mark:** Photograph the dial close-up showing the brand name, model name, and any sub-dial detail.
- **Condition:** Show the crystal (any scratches?), the bezel (fading, dings?), and the bracelet (stretch, scratches?).
- **Certificate:** Photograph the warranty card, COSC certificate, or service records if available.
- **Lighting:** Soft, diffused light from one side. A sheet of white paper curved behind the watch works well. Avoid flash — it obliterates dial detail.
- **What dealers need:** Model reference number, serial number, box and papers status, service history, and bracelet condition.

### Jewellery

**Essential viewTypes:** `overview`, `maker_mark`, `condition`, `certificate`, `scale_reference`

- **Overview:** Photograph on a plain dark background (black velvet is ideal for gold and diamonds).
- **Maker mark / hallmark:** Close-up of the hallmark. Use macro mode or the phone's zoom. If the hallmark is too small to read, photograph it anyway — dealers can often identify it from the shape.
- **Certificate:** GIA, AGS, or other grading reports for stones. Valuation certificates for insurance.
- **Scale reference:** Place a coin or ruler next to the piece to show size, especially for rings and brooches.
- **Lighting:** Avoid direct flash on gemstones — it creates blown-out highlights. Use window light or a desk lamp at a 45-degree angle.
- **What dealers need:** Metal type and fineness (from hallmark), stone details (from certificate), weight if known, and whether the item is complete (no missing stones).

### Art

**Essential viewTypes:** `overview`, `identification`, `provenance`, `maker_mark`, `condition`, `damage`

- **Overview:** Photograph the front of the work square-on, filling the frame. Avoid angles. If it is behind glass, turn off the flash and position yourself so no reflections are visible.
- **Identification:** Close-up of any title label, gallery label, or inscription on the front.
- **Provenance:** Photograph the entire back of the work — this is where gallery labels, exhibition stickers, auction lot labels, and framer stamps live. Take multiple shots if there are many labels.
- **Maker mark:** Artist signature, usually bottom-right on paintings, in pencil below the image on prints.
- **Condition / Damage:** Any cracks in the paint surface, foxing on paper, tears, water stains, or frame damage.
- **Lighting:** Even, indirect light. Photograph during the day near a window but out of direct sun. Never use flash on oil paintings — glare renders the image useless.
- **What dealers need:** Artist name, title if known, medium, dimensions (height x width), whether signed, condition, and provenance evidence.

### Coins

**Essential viewTypes:** `overview`, `identification`, `condition`, `certificate`, `scale_reference`

- **Overview:** Photograph both obverse (heads) and reverse (tails) individually.
- **Identification:** Close-up of the date, mint mark, and denomination.
- **Condition:** Even lighting that reveals surface wear, luster, and any marks or scratches.
- **Certificate:** If the coin is in a graded slab (NGC, PCGS), photograph the slab label showing the grade and certification number.
- **Scale reference:** A ruler alongside for oversized or undersized pieces.
- **Lighting:** Angled light from one side (about 30-45 degrees) brings out surface detail and luster. Direct overhead light flattens the image. Never use flash.
- **Background:** Plain black or dark gray.
- **What dealers need:** Country, date, denomination, mint mark, and condition/grade. For slabbed coins, the certification number is enough — everything else can be looked up.

### Stamps

**Essential viewTypes:** `overview`, `identification`, `condition`, `scale_reference`

- **Overview:** Photograph the stamp in its album page context if it is mounted, or individually on a dark background if loose.
- **Identification:** Face value, country name, and any overprint details.
- **Condition:** Show margins (for imperforate stamps), perforations, cancellation type and heaviness, gum condition (for mint stamps — photograph from the back).
- **Scale reference:** Important for stamps where size distinguishes varieties.
- **Lighting:** Flat, even lighting. Avoid shadows from album page edges. A lightbox or window light works well.
- **What dealers need:** SG or Scott number if known, mint or used status, gum condition (unmounted mint, lightly hinged, heavily hinged), centering quality, and any faults (thins, tears, creases — hold up to light to check).

### Vehicles

**Essential viewTypes:** `overview`, `identification`, `serial_number`, `condition`, `damage`, `environment`

- **Overview:** Four exterior shots — front, rear, driver side, passenger side. Plus two interior shots — dashboard and rear cabin.
- **Identification:** Close-up of the badge/model name and the registration plates.
- **Serial number:** VIN plate on the dashboard and/or door jamb sticker. Photograph the V5C document.
- **Condition:** Odometer reading, tire condition, engine bay.
- **Damage:** Any dents, scratches, rust, cracked glass, or interior wear.
- **Environment:** Where and how the vehicle is stored (garage, driveway, barn).
- **Lighting:** Daylight, overcast is best. Direct sun creates harsh shadows under body panels.
- **What dealers need:** Make, model, year, mileage, MOT status, service history, V5C, and number of keys.

### Firearms

**Essential viewTypes:** `overview`, `serial_number`, `maker_mark`, `condition`, `certificate`

- **Overview:** Full length of the firearm on a plain surface, unloaded, action open.
- **Serial number:** Close-up of the serial number on the receiver or frame.
- **Maker mark:** Manufacturer name, proof marks, barrel stamps.
- **Condition:** Stock condition (scratches, cracks, repairs), bore condition (if safe to photograph), blueing wear.
- **Certificate:** Photograph the relevant page of the FAC or SGC listing the firearm.
- **Lighting:** Indirect daylight. Flash tends to wash out engraving details.
- **What dealers need:** Make, model, caliber/gauge, serial number, barrel length, certificate details, and proof marks. Note that sales of firearms are tightly regulated — the executor should contact a registered firearms dealer early.

### Wine

**Essential viewTypes:** `overview`, `label`, `condition`, `environment`

- **Overview:** Front label clearly visible. For a collection, photograph groups of bottles organized by region or producer.
- **Label:** Close-up of the front label (chateau, vintage, appellation) and back label if present.
- **Condition:** Fill level (ullage), cork condition if visible, any label damage (staining, tearing, fading).
- **Environment:** Storage conditions — temperature-controlled cellar, wine fridge, rack in a spare room. This significantly affects value.
- **Lighting:** Gentle side lighting. Avoid flash — it reflects harshly off glass.
- **What dealers need:** Producer, vintage, appellation, bottle format, fill level, label condition, and storage history. Professional storage receipts from a bonded warehouse dramatically increase buyer confidence.

### Books

**Essential viewTypes:** `overview`, `identification`, `condition`, `damage`, `provenance`

- **Overview:** Front board (cover), spine, and rear board — three separate shots.
- **Identification:** Title page (the definitive identification for rare books — not the cover).
- **Condition:** Binding tightness (open the book to the middle and photograph — does it lie flat or spring shut?), foxing, browning, dampstaining.
- **Damage:** Any tears, detached pages, missing dust jacket panels, bumped corners.
- **Provenance:** Bookplates, inscriptions, bookseller tickets.
- **Lighting:** Even, indirect light. Avoid flash on glossy dust jackets.
- **What dealers need:** Author, title, publisher, date, edition statement, dust jacket presence and condition, and binding type.

### Musical Instruments

**Essential viewTypes:** `overview`, `serial_number`, `maker_mark`, `condition`, `damage`, `label`

- **Overview:** Full instrument from the front and back. Include the case if original.
- **Serial number:** Usually on the headstock (guitars), inside the body (violins — photograph through the f-hole), or on the frame (pianos).
- **Maker mark:** Brand name, luthier label, or manufacturer stamp.
- **Condition:** Fret wear (guitars), bow hair condition (strings), pad condition (woodwind), action height.
- **Damage:** Cracks in the soundboard, broken tuning pegs, dents in brass, water damage.
- **Label:** Internal label (violins, guitars) — use the phone torch to illuminate and photograph through the sound hole.
- **Lighting:** Soft daylight. Photograph instruments on a large flat surface to avoid them rolling or sliding.
- **What dealers need:** Make, model, serial number, year of manufacture, country of origin, playing condition, and case/accessories included.

### Furniture & Antiques

**Essential viewTypes:** `overview`, `maker_mark`, `condition`, `damage`, `provenance`, `scale_reference`

- **Overview:** Front view, side profile, and a top-down view if relevant (tables, desks). Clear the surface of personal items before photographing.
- **Maker mark:** Check drawer interiors, the underside, and the back for maker stamps, paper labels, or branded hardware.
- **Condition:** Patina, woodworm holes (tap the surrounding wood — active worm produces dust), drawer runners, hinges, locks.
- **Damage:** Veneer lifting, water rings, structural repairs, replaced handles.
- **Provenance:** Any auction house stickers, dealer labels, or family documentation.
- **Scale reference:** Include a person or doorway in the shot for context on large pieces.
- **Lighting:** Daylight from a window is ideal. Pull the piece away from the wall to photograph the back.
- **What dealers need:** Period or style, wood type, dimensions (height x width x depth), maker if known, condition, and whether any restoration has been done.

## Minimum Viable Documentation

If time or energy is limited, every single item in the estate should have at least these three photographs:

1. **Overview** (`viewType: overview`) — The whole item, clearly lit, on a plain background. This is the shot that identifies the item at a glance and serves as the primary image in any inventory.

2. **Identification** (`viewType: identification`) — Whatever makes this item specifically identifiable: the title page, the dial, the catalogue number, the label. If you had to describe the item to a dealer over the phone, this is the shot that replaces a thousand words.

3. **Condition** (`viewType: condition`) — An honest view of the item's current state. Do not hide damage — it will be discovered during valuation, and concealing it wastes everyone's time.

These three shots take less than a minute per item with a smartphone. They are sufficient for an initial inventory and can be supplemented later with specialist photographs (serial numbers, certificates, provenance) when needed.

## General Tips

- **Use a plain background.** A white sheet of paper, a clean table, or a dark cloth. Avoid photographing items on patterned carpets or cluttered surfaces.
- **Use natural light where possible.** A window on an overcast day provides ideal, even illumination. If you must use artificial light, bounce it off a white wall or ceiling.
- **Turn off the flash.** Flash creates harsh shadows and blown-out reflections on glass, metal, and glossy surfaces. The only exception is photographing the interior of a violin or guitar body, where the phone torch is helpful.
- **Clean the phone lens.** A fingerprint on the lens produces a haze that ruins close-up detail shots.
- **Fill the frame.** The item should take up most of the image. Crop tightly. Extra background is wasted resolution.
- **Hold steady.** For close-ups, brace your elbows on the table. Blurry macro shots are unusable.
- **Take more than you think you need.** Storage is free. A shot you did not take is a shot you cannot get back once the item has been moved or sold.
